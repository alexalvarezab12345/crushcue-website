## The Short Answer

When someone actively engages with your social media but doesn't text, they're orbiting — maintaining digital presence without making real contact. It keeps you aware of them, keeps them in your awareness, and costs them nothing.

## Reality Check

A like takes less than a second. It's the lowest-commitment form of contact available, and it's designed to be seen. When someone consistently shows up on your content but not in your messages, they're making a deliberate choice about how close they want to get — and how much they want to reveal.

## What's Actually Going On

**They want to stay on your radar without committing to anything.** The like is a signal that says "I'm here, I'm watching" without requiring a response or a real conversation. It's comfort with no risk.

**They're interested but conflict-avoidant.** Texting requires intention and vulnerability. Liking a post is passive. Some people who are interested but uncertain about the reception will orbit rather than reach out directly.

**They're keeping you as an option.** Particularly common with exes and situationship alumni — the like says "don't fully move on" without saying anything at all.

**They're genuinely just engaging with social media.** Less common when it's consistent and pattern-like, but possible. If they like everyone's posts at the same rate, this is probably nothing personal.

## What It Probably Means

The gap between social media engagement and direct contact is meaningful. Someone who wants to talk to you will talk to you. Likes without messages are a substitute for connection, not a form of it.

If the pattern involves someone you've had a previous connection with, the orbiting is almost always intentional — a way of maintaining presence without having to initiate a real conversation.

## What To Do Next

- Don't interpret likes as evidence of interest that needs to be acted on. They're ambient.
- If you want to know what they want, ask directly: "You engage with everything I post but never text — what's going on there?"
- If they're an ex: consider whether you want to close that loop by removing them from your close-friends list or muting their ability to see your stories.
- If you're hoping they'll convert the likes into a text — that rarely happens on its own.

## What Not To Do

- Don't post to send them a signal. That's performing for an audience of one and it keeps you emotionally stuck.
- Don't read individual likes as meaningful. Read the pattern.
- Don't wait for a like to become a text. If they wanted to text, they would have.
- Don't remove them dramatically hoping they'll notice and reach out. They might, but that's not a foundation.

## Common Questions

**Why would someone like all my posts but not text me?**

Because liking is easy and texting is vulnerable. A like says "I see you" without requiring anything back. For someone who's uncertain, conflict-avoidant, or wants presence without commitment, it's the perfect low-stakes move.

**Is it a sign they still like me?**

Maybe. But interest that doesn't convert into actual contact isn't actionable. What someone does with their interest matters more than whether they have it.

**Should I reach out since they're clearly paying attention?**

If you want to, yes — but go in with clear expectations. If they've had your number and haven't texted, a message from you might get a warm response but probably won't change the underlying dynamic.

**What does it mean when an ex likes everything I post?**

It means they're keeping an eye on you. Whether that means they want to reconnect or just don't want to fully let go is something only direct contact will clarify. Likes alone aren't enough to work with.

**How do I get them to stop orbiting if I want them to move on?**

The cleanest move is to make your content less visible to them — remove them from your close friends list on Instagram, or mute their ability to see your stories. You don't have to explain it.
