## The Short Answer

When someone cancels plans but keeps texting, they want the emotional connection without the accountability of actually showing up. The phone is safer — it's easier to control, easier to exit, and easier to maintain without real commitment.

## Reality Check

One cancellation is normal life. People get sick, work runs over, things happen. Two cancellations with the same category of excuse is worth paying attention to. Three is a pattern — and the pattern is more honest than the apology.

The part that makes this confusing is the texting that follows. They reach out, they check in, they seem warm and present. That's real — they do want connection. They just want it in the format that requires nothing from them.

## What's Actually Going On

**They're conflict-avoidant.** In-person interaction requires full presence and vulnerability. Texting lets them control the pace, manage their presentation, and exit at any moment. Some people will choose this indefinitely over the discomfort of actually showing up.

**They're not as interested as they seem.** Canceling is easy. Texting is also easy. Neither is the test. Showing up when they said they would is the test — and they're not passing it.

**They're using you as emotional support without reciprocating your investment.** They get the connection they need through texts. You're putting in the effort of showing up (or trying to) while they maintain comfort from a distance.

**Genuine circumstances that keep recurring.** Less common. Real life can be chaotic. But even truly busy people eventually find a window — and they usually show more urgency to reschedule when they're genuinely interested.

## What It Probably Means

The texts after the cancellation are not a sign that things are fine. They're a substitution. If you keep accepting that substitution, the pattern will continue — not because they're bad, but because it's working for them.

## What To Do Next

- Stop accepting texts as a consolation after a cancellation. Let the conversation rest.
- The next time they suggest a plan, let them pick the details and confirm the day before.
- If they cancel again, don't immediately re-engage in warm text conversation. Create space instead.
- Name it if you want: "I've noticed we make plans but they keep falling through — I need things to actually happen, not just get rescheduled."

## What Not To Do

- Don't keep rescheduling without acknowledging the pattern. You're training them that cancellation has no cost.
- Don't treat the apologetic texts as evidence that they're committed. Actions, not words.
- Don't have a major conversation over text — particularly about this pattern. If you're going to name it, do it in a medium that actually requires them to show up.
- Don't give unlimited benefit of the doubt. Empathy is appropriate; indefinite tolerance of a pattern isn't.

## Common Questions

**Why would someone keep canceling if they actually like me?**

Because they like the idea of you, or the connection, more than they're willing to invest in actually building it. Some people are genuinely avoidant of the vulnerability that comes with real in-person presence — especially when there's emotional stakes.

**Should I stop making plans with them?**

Not necessarily — but stop carrying all the initiative. Let them suggest the next plan, set the details, and confirm it. If they do, great. If they don't, you've learned something important about who's actually invested.

**What does it mean if they cancel but immediately want to call or FaceTime?**

It means they want connection in the format that gives them control. A call can be ended with a button. They canceled the plan that required them to leave their house, commit to a time, and be physically present. The call is the easier version of what they said they wanted.

**Is this a red flag?**

A repeated pattern of cancellations with no change in behavior after being named is a red flag. A single cancellation is normal. Two with a genuine reschedule that happens is fine. Three cancellations in a pattern is information.

**How do I stop feeling okay with this?**

By getting honest with yourself about what you actually need. If you need someone who shows up when they say they will, and this person consistently doesn't, that's a fundamental mismatch — regardless of how warm they are over text.
