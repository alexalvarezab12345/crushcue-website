## The Short Answer

Not being sure if someone likes you is one of the most universal experiences in dating — and usually more solvable than it feels. Most signals become readable with the right frame.

## Reality Check

Crush confusion is mostly a result of trying to interpret ambiguous actions through the lens of what you're hoping for. The signals exist — but you need to read them without the distortion of wanting a specific answer.

## What's Actually Going On

Interest tends to show in a pattern across multiple signals, not one decisive moment. Someone who likes you usually initiates sometimes, responds warmly, remembers things you've said, and creates opportunities to see you. Someone who doesn't usually does fewer of those things even when the conversation is friendly.

Here are the most common versions of this situation:

- **They flirt constantly but never escalate** — Friendly is baseline, but do they seek you out? Do they remember details? Those are the differentiators.
- **Long texts and deep conversations but nothing romantic** — One flirty comment isn't a declaration. Is it a pattern or a one-off? Pattern matters more.
- **They act differently with the reader than with others** — Track it for a week or two. If you always initiate and they always respond warmly but never start it, that's useful data.
- **Confusing physical closeness with emotional distance** — Third-party intel is notoriously unreliable. If you want to know, the most accurate signal is from them directly.

## What It Probably Means

If you're consistently unsure, one of two things is happening: they're interested but cautious, or they're being friendly without romantic intent. A direct move — suggesting a plan, or asking where things stand — converts the ambiguity into actual information.

## What To Do Next

- List the signals you've noticed — initiation, warmth, memory, effort. Count more than feel.
- Look for patterns over days and weeks, not individual moments.
- Test interest by suggesting something specific: "We should get coffee sometime." Their enthusiasm level is informative.
- If you want to be more direct, you can — the risk of asking is much lower than the cost of months of uncertainty.
- Trust what you observe more than what you hope.

## What Not To Do

- Don't spend months trying to decode indirect signals when one direct move would tell you.
- Don't over-interpret every interaction as confirmation of what you want to believe.
- Don't try to make them like you more before making a move — either they're interested or they're not.
- Don't tell yourself the timing isn't right indefinitely. There's rarely a perfect moment.

## Common Questions

**How do I know if someone likes me or is just being friendly?**

Watch for patterns across multiple signals, not single moments: consistent initiation, remembering small details, finding reasons to be near you or include you. One flirty comment isn't a declaration — a pattern of them is.

**How do I find out if my crush likes me without asking directly?**

Suggest something low-stakes — coffee, a shared activity, a specific invite. Their enthusiasm level gives you a read on interest without forcing a big conversation.

**How long should I wait before making a move on my crush?**

Usually a few weeks to a couple of months before taking a small risk. Much longer than that and you're burning time on uncertainty that a simple move could resolve.

**Is it better to just ask my crush if they like me?**

Yes — ask directly or make a small move. The answer you get from direct action is more useful than any amount of indirect observation. The risk is much lower than months of uncertainty.

**What does it mean if I can't tell if my crush is interested?**

Either the signals are genuinely mixed — they might be uncertain too — or the signals are friendly but not romantic. Either way, a small direct move converts ambiguity into real information faster than observation will.
