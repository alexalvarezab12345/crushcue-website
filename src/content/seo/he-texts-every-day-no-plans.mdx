## The Short Answer

Someone who texts you every day but never commits to actually seeing you enjoys the connection — on their terms. It's low-effort intimacy: all the warmth of closeness without the vulnerability of showing up. That's a pattern worth naming.

## Reality Check

Daily texts feel significant. They create a rhythm that mimics something real. But texting is the lowest-commitment form of contact — it requires no scheduling, no effort, no showing up. If someone genuinely wants to be in your life, they will eventually make something happen.

## What's Actually Going On

There are three common reasons someone texts every day without ever making plans:

**They're keeping you warm.** You're important enough to maintain, not important enough to prioritize. The texts are the relationship — they have no intention of building beyond that.

**They're conflict-avoidant.** Suggesting plans requires risking rejection. Some people will maintain indefinite textual intimacy rather than risk the vulnerability of asking someone out directly.

**They genuinely haven't noticed the gap.** Less common, but real — some people compartmentalize texting and seeing someone as completely separate activities. A direct suggestion usually resolves this one fast.

## What It Probably Means

The pattern is more useful than any single message. If you've suggested hanging out more than once and it's never materialized, that's your information. Interest that exists but never converts into plans is interest with conditions attached — usually the condition that you do all the pursuing.

## What To Do Next

- Make one clear, specific suggestion: not "we should hang" but "are you free Thursday evening?"
- If they say yes but go vague on details, follow up once with a specific time or place.
- If they deflect again, stop initiating for a week or two. Don't explain it — just stop.
- Watch what happens. Someone who notices and reaches out is paying attention. Someone who doesn't isn't.

## What Not To Do

- Don't keep soft-suggesting plans and accepting soft agreement as progress.
- Don't mistake daily texting for commitment. Volume isn't depth.
- Don't have an ultimatum conversation before giving them a clear, direct invitation first.
- Don't assume malice — but don't ignore the pattern either.

## Common Questions

**Why does he text me every day if he doesn't want to see me?**

Daily texts cost nothing and feel good. Some people maintain textual relationships because it gives them the emotional benefit of connection without the responsibility of actually showing up. It's not always intentional — but the effect is the same.

**Should I bring it up directly?**

Yes, but frame it as a question, not an accusation. "I feel like we talk every day but never actually see each other — is that something you want to change?" gives them room to be honest. Their answer, and what they do after, tells you everything.

**What if he says he's been busy?**

Busy people still make plans. They schedule further out, they give shorter windows, but they eventually say "I'm free on Saturday, you?" If the busy excuse has lasted more than a few weeks, it's not the reason.

**Is this a situationship?**

It can be. A situationship is often defined by exactly this — regular contact, emotional investment, no formal structure or in-person relationship. If it feels like a relationship but has no dates in it, that's worth examining.

**Should I stop texting back to get him to chase?**

Not as a game, but stopping the one-sided effort is useful data-gathering. If you stop initiating and the texts go quiet, you've learned something important. If they reach out and suggest plans, you've also learned something important.
