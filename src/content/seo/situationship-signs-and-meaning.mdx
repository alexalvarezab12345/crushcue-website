# Situationship Signs: What It Means and What To Do

_By CrushCue Editorial Team — Published June 17, 2026_

## The Short Answer

A situationship is a relationship that functions like one in practice but has never been named or agreed to. The lack of label isn't freedom — it's usually just a way to keep things indefinitely uncertain.

## Reality Check

Situationships feel like relationships because they have most of the ingredients: time, intimacy, inside jokes, maybe even feelings. The part that's missing is the mutual decision to actually be in one. That missing piece matters more than the feelings that are present.

## What's Actually Going On

Most situationships form by drift, not design. You start hanging out, it gets comfortable, nobody asks the question, and suddenly months have passed. The person who's more attached suffers. The person who's more avoidant gets what they want without having to commit to it.

Here are the most common versions of this situation:

- **We talk daily but we're not officially dating** — You've built a relationship-shaped thing with an escape hatch. Whose escape hatch is it, really?
- **We hooked up and kept hanging out but there's no label** — This asymmetry has a name: they want the experience without the accountability.
- **They say they're not ready for a relationship but act like we're in one** — The longer it goes undefined, the harder it gets to ask. But also: the longer it goes, the more it costs you.
- **Everyone assumes we're together but we've never had the talk** — Watch the behavior, not the words. They're already in one; they just won't call it that.

## What It Probably Means

A situationship is someone having it both ways. You get connection-like experiences in exchange for accepting indefinite uncertainty. The only way to change it is to name what you actually want and let them respond.

## What To Do Next

- Get clear on what you want — not what you'll settle for. Write it down.
- Have the conversation when you're calm and the moment isn't already charged.
- Say what you want directly: "I want to be in a relationship with you" or "I want us to define this."
- Give them time to answer — a day or two, not a week. The answer should come fairly easily if it's yes.
- Take their answer at face value. Don't negotiate someone into wanting what you want.

## What Not To Do

- Don't give ultimatums in the heat of an argument. It turns a conversation into a confrontation.
- Don't accept "I don't know" as a permanent answer — it can be a temporary one for a week, not indefinitely.
- Don't keep deepening the situationship while waiting for it to define itself. It won't.
- Don't convince yourself the label doesn't matter if it does. It matters because it tells you where you stand.

## Common Questions

**Can a situationship turn into a real relationship?**

Yes, but only if someone initiates a direct conversation about what they want. It doesn't happen by waiting and hoping.

**Is it okay to stay in a situationship if I'm happy?**

Only if you're genuinely happy with the current arrangement and not just tolerating it. If you're reading this, you probably aren't.

**How long is too long to be in a situationship without clarity?**

A few weeks to a couple of months is room to figure things out. Six months or longer with no movement is a pattern, not a phase.

**What do they actually want if they act like we're together but won't commit?**

Their actions tell you they want what's happening now. Their words suggest they don't want to commit to it. Believe both: they want this but not more.

**Can I end a situationship without drama?**

Yes — you can end it, redefine it, or decide you're okay with the ambiguity. You don't need their permission to make a decision about your own situation.
