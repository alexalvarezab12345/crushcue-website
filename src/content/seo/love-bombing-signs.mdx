## The Short Answer

Love bombing is when someone overwhelms you with affection, attention, and intensity very early in a relationship — before either of you actually knows each other well enough to justify it. It feels incredible at first. That's the point.

## Reality Check

Genuine attraction does create excitement, and some people are naturally expressive and enthusiastic. But there's a difference between someone who's genuinely excited about you and someone who's running a pattern — consciously or not — designed to create rapid dependency.

Love bombing typically involves: constant contact, extreme compliments, fast-tracking intimacy, future-planning within weeks, and a subtle pressure to match their intensity. When you slow down or pull back, their response tells you everything.

## What's Actually Going On

Love bombing usually comes from one of a few places:

**Genuine intensity with poor emotional regulation.** They feel things fast, don't pace themselves, and often burn out just as quickly. The bombing isn't manipulative — it's just unsustainable.

**Conscious or unconscious control tactics.** Overwhelming someone with affection early creates emotional debt and dependency. Once you're attached, they have leverage. This is the more concerning version.

**Unresolved anxious attachment.** They need immediate, intense closeness to feel secure, and they create it artificially by flooding the early relationship with intensity.

## What It Probably Means

If everything feels too perfect too soon, your instinct to question it is correct. Healthy connection builds over time as two people learn about each other. What can't be built in two weeks is genuine knowledge of who someone is. Someone claiming to deeply know you in week one is reacting to an idea of you, not you.

## What To Do Next

- Deliberately slow down the pace. Respond less frequently, suggest seeing each other less often than they want.
- Pay close attention to their reaction. Someone genuinely interested in you will adjust. Someone love bombing will push back, guilt-trip, or withdraw to punish you.
- Don't let the intensity create obligation. You don't owe someone an intense relationship because they're offering one.
- Ask yourself: does this feel like connection, or like pressure?

## What Not To Do

- Don't dismiss the good feelings entirely — some of it might be real. But don't let the good feelings override your observations.
- Don't match the intensity just because it's being offered. You set your own pace.
- Don't explain your need for space as a flaw. That need is normal and healthy.
- Don't assume it will always feel this way. The bombing phase is temporary by nature.

## Common Questions

**How do I know if it's love bombing or just someone who's really into me?**

The clearest test is your reaction to slowing down. Tell them you need a bit more space this week — fewer texts, less intensity. If they're genuinely into you, they'll respect it. If they're love bombing, they'll escalate pressure, become hurt, or suddenly withdraw to punish you for pulling back.

**Can love bombing happen unintentionally?**

Yes. Some people genuinely feel things this intensely at the start of a relationship without understanding the effect it has. That doesn't make the dynamic less problematic, but it does mean they might be open to feedback if you name it directly.

**What happens after the love bombing phase?**

It always ends. What replaces it tells you what you're actually dealing with. In a healthy version, it settles into something quieter but consistent. In a problematic version, it's replaced by withdrawal, criticism, or control — which creates a cycle where you're chasing the early intensity.

**Is love bombing a sign of a narcissist?**

Not necessarily. Narcissistic people do often love bomb, but so do people with anxious attachment, people with poor emotional regulation, and genuinely enthusiastic people who are just moving too fast. The pattern matters more than the label.

**Should I bring it up with them?**

Yes, if you want to give it a chance. Say something like: "Things have felt really intense really fast and I need to slow down a bit — that doesn't mean I'm not interested." How they handle that sentence is more informative than anything they've said so far.
